About the Adams Morgan Neighborhood

Adams Morgan is arguably Washington's most famous nightlife and dining district, a dense strip of restaurants, bars, and live music venues along 18th Street that draws visitors from across the region every weekend. Named for two once-segregated schools that shared the neighborhood — John Quincy Adams and Thomas P. Morgan — the area has long been one of DC's most internationally diverse neighborhoods, home to immigrant communities from Central America, Ethiopia, and beyond, reflected in its restaurant scene.

Beyond the nightlife strip, Adams Morgan is also home to colorful murals, independent bookstores, and the Marie Reed Recreation Center, giving it a genuine residential character behind the weekend crowds. Its proximity to Kalorama and Rock Creek Park adds a quieter, leafier edge to a neighborhood best known for its bars, and the mix of longtime residents and a steady rotation of renters gives Adams Morgan one of the most eclectic customer bases in the city.

The neighborhood's annual Adams Morgan Day festival, running since the 1970s, draws tens of thousands of visitors to 18th Street for food, music, and vendor stalls, and remains one of DC's most beloved neighborhood traditions. That same festival energy carries through much of the year on a smaller scale, with 18th Street's bars and restaurants staying busy well past midnight most weekends — a rhythm that shapes both the neighborhood's identity and District Cure's understanding of when and why Adams Morgan customers shop.

Housing in Adams Morgan skews heavily toward apartment buildings and English basement rentals rather than single-family homes, and turnover is higher here than in more residential neighborhoods nearby. That means District Cure's Adams Morgan customer base includes a meaningful share of newer DC transplants who are learning the District's cannabis registration system for the first time, alongside longtime residents who've watched the neighborhood's nightlife scene evolve over decades.

The neighborhood's murals, many painted by local and international artists over the past two decades, have become a destination in their own right, drawing photographers and casual visitors even on weekday afternoons when the bars are quiet. That artistic identity, paired with the neighborhood's deep culinary diversity, gives Adams Morgan a daytime character that's easy to overlook given its reputation as a nightlife hub — a side of the neighborhood District Cure's daytime customers know well.

The neighborhood's international restaurant scene also shapes purchasing patterns in subtle ways — District Cure staff report frequent requests for sativa or hybrid strains described as good for "a long dinner" among customers planning an evening at one of 18th Street's many multi-course restaurants, a use case distinct from the quicker, bar-hopping pattern more common among younger visitors.

Legal Access & Medical Cannabis Registration

Adams Morgan's mix of DC residents and out-of-town visitors makes District Cure's familiarity with all three DC registration pathways especially useful here. DC residents 21 and older can self-certify online, out-of-state medical cardholders can use reciprocity with a valid ID, and visitors without either can apply for a temporary non-resident registration, which involves a modest processing fee and is typically issued digitally within a short window after payment. Because so many Adams Morgan customers are visiting DC for the weekend rather than living here full time, District Cure's staff are especially practiced at walking newcomers through the registration process quickly, without slowing down an evening's plans. That familiarity with all three pathways has made District Cure a dependable option for anyone in Adams Morgan, whether they've lived in DC for twenty years or are visiting for a single weekend.

Cannabis Use in Washington DC: By the Numbers

DC's adult cannabis use rate — around 33% — reflects a jurisdiction where cannabis has been woven into everyday and social life for years, a pattern visible in nightlife-heavy neighborhoods like Adams Morgan. Nationally, support for legalizing cannabis has reached roughly 87% of Americans according to recent polling, and cannabis tax revenue nationwide has climbed to an estimated $25 billion annually, nearly double alcohol's tax contribution — numbers that track closely with how normalized responsible cannabis use has become in social settings across cities like Washington. The 2024 National Survey on Drug Use and Health found that roughly 22% of Americans age 12 and older reported past-year cannabis use, a figure DC's own nightlife-heavy neighborhoods like Adams Morgan help push well above the national average. That gap reflects decades of DC's cannabis culture being woven into its restaurant and bar scene long before most states legalized anything at all.
