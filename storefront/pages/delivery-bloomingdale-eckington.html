About the Bloomingdale & Eckington Neighborhood

Bloomingdale is a historic rowhouse neighborhood built up in the late 1800s and early 1900s, known today for its wide, tree-lined streets, the beloved Big Bear Cafe, and its proximity to the massive McMillan Reservoir and Sand Filtration Site, a century-old engineering landmark slowly being redeveloped into parkland and mixed-use space. The neighborhood has one of DC's most engaged community gardening and local-food cultures, anchored by Crispus Attucks Park, a hidden green space tucked behind the neighborhood's rowhouses.

Eckington, just to the east, shares much of Bloomingdale's rowhouse character but has historically been more industrial and working-class, with warehouse and light-manufacturing buildings gradually converting to apartments and offices as the NoMa development boom pushes northward from Union Station. Both neighborhoods sit near the NoMa-Gallaudet U Metro station, giving residents a direct Red Line connection into downtown while retaining a quieter, more residential feel than NoMa itself.

Bloomingdale's transformation over the past fifteen years has been especially dramatic — a neighborhood once considered a rough patch of the city is now among its most competitive for home buyers, with rowhouses that sold for a fraction of today's prices in the early 2000s. The McMillan Sand Filtration Site redevelopment, long stalled by community disputes over scale and use, has slowly moved forward with plans for parkland, retail, and housing that residents hope will finally activate one of the neighborhood's largest underused parcels.

Eckington's identity remains more industrial at its edges, with active rail lines and light-manufacturing buildings still visible even as residential conversion continues. That mix of old infrastructure and new residential development gives the neighborhood a slightly grittier, more transitional feel than Bloomingdale, though the two are close enough geographically that many residents consider them a single extended community.

Crispus Attucks Park, a community-run green space tucked mid-block behind Bloomingdale's rowhouses, exemplifies the neighborhood's do-it-yourself civic spirit — residents converted the former alley lot into public art space and gathering grounds largely through volunteer effort. That same grassroots energy shows up in the neighborhood's active listservs and community meetings, where residents debate everything from zoning changes to new business openings with genuine investment in the outcome.

Best-Selling Cannabis Products in This Area

District Cure sees a strong mix of shopping habits from Bloomingdale and Eckington, reflecting neighborhoods in transition. Longtime rowhouse residents tend toward classic full-size flower purchases and CBD-forward wellness products, while the growing population of newer renters in Eckington's converted apartment buildings lean toward vape cartridges, pre-rolls, and grab-and-go edibles that fit smaller living spaces.

Gummies and other edibles remain consistently popular across both neighborhoods, with 5mg to 10mg options serving as the entry point for newer patients and higher-dose products favored by more experienced consumers. District Cure's staff note that Bloomingdale customers in particular ask thoughtful questions about sourcing and cultivation, echoing the neighborhood's community-garden culture and interest in where products come from.

Concentrates and specialty flower see moderate demand from a smaller, more dedicated group of repeat customers in both neighborhoods, while topicals are a reliable seller among Bloomingdale's older homeowning population managing everyday joint and muscle discomfort. Pre-roll multi-packs are especially popular with Eckington's younger renter population, who tend to buy in bulk for convenience rather than making frequent single-item trips.

As both neighborhoods continue to grow and attract new residents from across the region, District Cure's Georgia Avenue location has become a natural stop on the way in or out of the area, particularly for residents commuting along North Capitol Street or Rhode Island Avenue.

Big Bear Cafe's role as Bloomingdale's unofficial community living room extends, in a sense, to how residents talk about local businesses generally — recommendations spread by word of mouth over coffee rather than through advertising, and District Cure's reputation in the neighborhood has grown largely the same way. Staff report that new Bloomingdale customers frequently mention being referred by a neighbor rather than finding the dispensary through a search engine or map app.

Eckington's newer apartment residents, many of whom relocated to DC for work in government, tech, or nonprofit sectors, tend to be first-time patients navigating DC's registration system for the first time. District Cure's staff have grown accustomed to walking these newer residents through the self-certification process step by step, a service that's become as much a part of the neighborhood's reputation as the products themselves.

Legal Access & Medical Cannabis Registration

Bloomingdale and Eckington residents register the same way as the rest of DC — through the ABCA medical cannabis program. Adults 21 and older can self-certify online without a physician's visit, and the process typically results in a usable temporary registration the same day. The neighborhoods' proximity to North Capitol Street makes the drive to Georgia Avenue simple, and the NoMa-Gallaudet Metro connects to bus routes that reach the dispensary as well. Newer residents who moved to Eckington's converted apartment buildings from out of state can also use reciprocity if they still hold an active medical card from their previous home, simplifying the transition to DC's system. District Cure's staff regularly help these newer arrivals understand the difference between reciprocity and full DC self-certification, since many eventually switch to a DC registration once they've settled into the neighborhood permanently.

Cannabis Use in Washington DC: By the Numbers

DC's registered patient base has grown to more than 116,000 as of April 2026, a figure that includes a steadily rising share of newer residents in redeveloping neighborhoods like Eckington and Bloomingdale. Nationally, the cannabis industry is projected to support nearly 800,000 jobs by 2029, and the shift toward regulated, lab-tested products has paralleled the same kind of neighborhood transformation happening in Bloomingdale — from an under-the-radar rowhouse community to one of the city's most in-demand residential areas, now served by a fully licensed dispensary market rather than an unregulated gifting economy. DC's overall adult cannabis use rate, estimated around a third of residents, remains among the highest of any U.S. jurisdiction, a pattern that holds true across both Bloomingdale's established rowhouse blocks and Eckington's newer apartment developments alike. As both neighborhoods continue to attract new residents, that usage pattern is likely to remain steady rather than shift dramatically in either direction over the next several years as both neighborhoods finish their current wave of redevelopment and continue to attract new residents from across the region.
