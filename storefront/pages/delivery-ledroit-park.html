About the Howard University & LeDroit Park Neighborhood

Howard University, one of the nation's most prominent historically Black universities, anchors this section of Northwest DC with a campus that has produced generations of doctors, lawyers, artists, and civic leaders. The neighboring LeDroit Park community, developed in the 1870s as one of DC's first planned suburbs, is known for its ornate Victorian and Second Empire-style homes, many now protected as part of a historic district that predates much of the surrounding city.

The area's cultural weight is hard to overstate — the historic Howard Theatre, once a cornerstone of the U Street "Black Broadway" jazz era, sits just blocks away, and the neighborhood's identity is deeply tied to Howard's role as an intellectual and cultural hub for Black America. Today the area blends longtime homeowners, Howard faculty, and a rotating population of students, giving it a distinct rhythm tied to the academic calendar.

LeDroit Park itself was originally built as a whites-only enclave in the 1870s, physically separated from the surrounding Black neighborhoods by a fence that stood until the 1890s, when residents tore it down in protest. That history makes the neighborhood's current identity — predominantly Black, deeply tied to Howard University, and fiercely protective of its historic housing stock — a meaningful reversal of its original design. Many of the neighborhood's Victorian homes have been carefully restored, and LeDroit Park today functions as a kind of quiet, tree-lined retreat immediately adjacent to one of the busiest sections of Georgia Avenue.

The rhythm of the neighborhood shifts noticeably with Howard's academic calendar — move-in weekends, homecoming, and graduation all bring surges of foot traffic and energy to the area, while summer months are noticeably quieter. That cyclical pattern extends to local businesses along Georgia Avenue, including District Cure, which sees predictable swings in younger customer traffic tied to the university schedule.

Howard's influence on the surrounding neighborhood extends well beyond its own gates — the university's hospital, law school, and divinity school all draw a steady stream of professionals, patients, and visitors to the area, adding yet another layer to the neighborhood's daily foot traffic beyond the undergraduate population. LeDroit Park's own historic marker program, maintained by neighborhood preservationists, walks visitors through the area's role in DC's Black history, from its origins as a segregated enclave to its current identity as one of the city's most architecturally protected Black neighborhoods.

Best-Selling Cannabis Products in This Area

Given the university population nearby, District Cure sees strong demand for value-oriented flower and pre-roll multi-packs — products that offer reliable effects without a premium price tag, appealing to a customer base that includes graduate students and young professionals on a budget. Hybrid and sativa-leaning strains are especially popular for daytime or study-adjacent use among the 21-and-older crowd who are registered patients.

Edibles, particularly gummies, are a major category here, prized for their discretion in dorm-adjacent housing and shared apartments where smoking isn't practical. Vape cartridges are similarly popular for the same reason — low odor, easy to store, and simple to use without drawing attention in shared living situations near campus.

LeDroit Park's longtime homeowners, by contrast, tend to shop more like Petworth or Park View residents — favoring full-size flower purchases, CBD topicals for everyday aches, and slower-onset tinctures for sleep and relaxation. This creates an interesting split in District Cure's customer base near this stretch of Georgia Avenue: younger, value-focused university-adjacent shoppers on one side, and established, wellness-focused LeDroit Park residents on the other.

Concentrates see modest but consistent demand from a smaller group of more experienced consumers in the area, and District Cure's staff regularly field questions from newly-registered younger patients about dosing basics — a reminder of how important clear, judgment-free education is in a neighborhood with such a wide range of cannabis experience levels.

Seasonal swings tied to the academic calendar are especially visible in this part of Georgia Avenue: sales of pre-roll multi-packs and lower-cost flower options rise noticeably during the fall and spring semesters, while summer months see a shift back toward LeDroit Park's steadier, homeowner-driven buying patterns of full-size flower and wellness-focused CBD products.

District Cure's staff have also noticed that Howard-affiliated customers, including graduate students and faculty, tend to ask more detailed questions about cannabinoid content and lab testing than the broader university population — a pattern staff attribute to the campus's strong health sciences and pharmacy programs, whose students and faculty bring a more clinical curiosity to their own cannabis purchases.

Legal Access & Medical Cannabis Registration

All cannabis sales near Howard University run through DC's licensed medical program. Because self-certification does not require a physician's recommendation for residents 21 and older, many students who meet DC residency and age requirements can register in minutes through the ABCA portal. Students who are 18 to 20 years old are not eligible for self-certification and must submit a healthcare provider recommendation with their application — an important distinction for the university's younger undergraduate population. Faculty and staff who commute from outside DC can use reciprocity with a valid out-of-state medical card, and District Cure's team is well versed in helping the full range of Howard-affiliated customers navigate whichever pathway applies to them.

Cannabis Use in Washington DC: By the Numbers

DC's registered medical cannabis patient population surpassed 116,000 by April 2026, and national data from the 2024 National Survey on Drug Use and Health shows roughly 22% of Americans age 12 and older reported past-year cannabis use — a figure DC's own usage rate significantly outpaces at around 33% of adults. College-adjacent neighborhoods like the area around Howard University tend to reflect national trends showing cannabis use holding steady or rising slightly among young adults, even as growth nationally has begun to plateau according to recent Gallup polling. DC's roughly 68 licensed medical retailers, including District Cure, now operate under lab-testing and labeling standards that didn't exist under the District's earlier unregulated gifting model, giving university-affiliated customers clearer information about potency and sourcing than was previously available. That transparency matters to a campus community with a strong tradition of health sciences education and critical inquiry.
