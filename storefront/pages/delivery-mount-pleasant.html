About the Mount Pleasant Neighborhood

Mount Pleasant is one of Washington's oldest streetcar suburbs, developed in the late 1800s as a genteel retreat from downtown and still known today for its Beaux-Arts and Victorian rowhouses along tree-lined streets. The neighborhood's commercial heart, Mount Pleasant Street, hosts one of the city's most beloved farmers markets every Saturday, alongside longtime Latin American and Ethiopian restaurants that reflect the area's decades-long role as a hub for DC's immigrant communities.

Bordering Rock Creek Park to the west and the National Zoo just beyond, Mount Pleasant has a distinctly green, almost suburban feel despite sitting less than three miles from the White House. The neighborhood has a strong civic culture, with an active neighborhood association and a reputation for resisting rapid commercial turnover, which has helped it retain an artsy, slightly bohemian character even as much of the surrounding city has changed.

Mount Pleasant Street itself remains one of the last stretches in Northwest DC where independent, family-owned businesses still outnumber chains — a deliberate outcome of decades of organizing by the neighborhood association to resist the kind of rapid commercial turnover that reshaped nearby Columbia Heights. That same instinct toward independence and community control shapes how residents think about local retail generally, including cannabis: Mount Pleasant shoppers tend to prefer a dispensary that feels like part of the neighborhood fabric rather than a chain operation.

The neighborhood is also one of DC's most linguistically and culturally diverse, with large Salvadoran, Vietnamese, and Ethiopian populations that have called Mount Pleasant home for generations. That diversity shows up in everything from the farmers market vendors to the restaurant scene along Mount Pleasant Street, and it extends to a customer base at District Cure that brings a wide range of cultural relationships with cannabis, from longstanding traditional uses to more recent recreational interest.

Mount Pleasant's row of Beaux-Arts apartment buildings along 16th Street, built in the early 1900s for federal workers, remain some of the most architecturally distinctive residential structures in the city, and many are now protected under historic district designation. That preservation-minded character extends to how the neighborhood approaches new commercial tenants generally: residents tend to favor businesses that feel rooted in the neighborhood's identity rather than imported from elsewhere, a bar that District Cure has worked to clear through consistent presence and genuine community engagement.

Best-Selling Cannabis Products in This Area

Mount Pleasant's customer base tends to be curious and open to trying new products, and District Cure sees strong interest here in specialty and small-batch flower alongside the standard indica, sativa, and hybrid categories. Customers frequently ask budtenders about terpene profiles and growing methods in more depth than average, reflecting the neighborhood's food-and-farmers-market culture translating into how residents think about cannabis.

Edibles sell well across the board here, with a notable interest in low-dose, precisely measured products — Mount Pleasant customers often want control over their experience rather than maximum potency. Chocolate and gummy edibles in the 5mg range are consistent sellers, and CBD-THC balanced tinctures see solid demand from a neighborhood with a sizable population of long-term residents managing chronic conditions.

Pre-rolls remain popular for residents heading out to Rock Creek Park trails or a neighborhood gathering, and vape cartridges see steady interest from younger renters near the 16th Street and Mount Pleasant Street corridors. Concentrates are a smaller share of Mount Pleasant's sales overall, but the neighborhood's more experienced consumers who do buy them tend to be knowledgeable, repeat customers who appreciate District Cure's rotating selection of live resin and rosin.

What distinguishes Mount Pleasant shoppers is the amount of conversation they want with staff — questions about sourcing, cultivation practice, and product transparency come up often, and District Cure's willingness to answer them in detail has built real loyalty in a neighborhood that generally prefers independent, well-informed businesses over anonymous transactions.

District Cure's staff also note that Mount Pleasant customers are more likely than average to ask about organic or sustainably grown flower, and to compare notes on specific cultivators the way other neighborhoods might compare notes on a favorite coffee roaster. That level of engagement has made the store's rotating small-batch selection a genuine draw, rather than just a secondary category behind the standard flower and edible lines.

The neighborhood's farmers market culture also shapes buying habits around timing — Saturday mornings and early afternoons see a noticeable spike in Mount Pleasant traffic at District Cure, as market-goers extend their weekly shopping trip to include a cannabis stop before heading home. Staff have come to expect this rhythm and often set aside extra time on Saturdays for the neighborhood's more conversation-heavy, question-driven customers.

Legal Access & Medical Cannabis Registration

Mount Pleasant residents access cannabis the same way every DC resident does — through ABCA's medical cannabis program. Self-certification lets adults 21 and older register online without a doctor's recommendation, typically within about ten minutes, and District Cure accepts both new temporary registrations and out-of-state reciprocity cards. The short trip down 16th Street or Mount Pleasant Street to Georgia Avenue makes the dispensary an easy addition to a farmers market run or weekend errand. Non-English-speaking residents in the neighborhood's Salvadoran and Ethiopian communities can also complete the ABCA application with translated instructions, and District Cure's staff are accustomed to walking multi-generational households through the registration process together. That patience and attention to detail has become part of the store's reputation in a neighborhood where word of mouth carries real weight.

Cannabis Use in Washington DC: By the Numbers

DC's cannabis use rate — an estimated one in three adults — places it second only to Vermont nationally, and the District's registered patient population has grown to more than 116,000 as of spring 2026. Mount Pleasant's demographic mix of longtime residents, young professionals, and families mirrors the broader national trend Gallup has tracked: active marijuana use among U.S. adults has climbed from roughly 14% in 2021-2022 to about 15% today, with usage patterns increasingly normalized across age groups and neighborhoods much like Mount Pleasant's own multi-generational population. Support for cannabis legalization nationally has reached roughly 87% of Americans, a figure that helps explain why a neighborhood built around civic engagement and community organizing has embraced a licensed dispensary as simply another neighborhood business rather than a point of controversy. That acceptance mirrors how quickly the neighborhood embraced its farmers market and community garden programs decades earlier.
