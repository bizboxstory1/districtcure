About the U Street Corridor & Cardozo Neighborhood

U Street earned its "Black Broadway" nickname in the early 20th century as home to the Lincoln Theatre, the Bohemian Caverns jazz club, and countless venues that hosted Duke Ellington, who grew up in the neighborhood, along with generations of Black musicians, writers, and civil rights leaders. The corridor's cultural significance runs deep, and much of that history is preserved today alongside a modern wave of restaurants, bars, and music venues that have made U Street one of DC's premier nightlife destinations once again.

Ben's Chili Bowl, open since 1958, remains the neighborhood's most iconic landmark, having survived the 1968 riots and decades of change while continuing to serve as a community gathering spot. The surrounding Cardozo area blends historic rowhouses with newer apartment development, and the neighborhood's Metro station on the Green and Yellow Lines keeps it firmly connected to both downtown and the Georgia Avenue corridor to the north.

The 1968 riots following Dr. Martin Luther King Jr.'s assassination hit U Street especially hard, and the corridor spent decades recovering economically even as its cultural memory remained a point of neighborhood pride. The opening of the U Street Metro station in the early 1990s helped spark the corridor's revival, and today the strip is once again one of DC's most vibrant nightlife destinations, with a self-conscious effort by many venues and cultural institutions to honor the neighborhood's jazz-era history even amid new development.

Cardozo, the residential area surrounding the nightlife strip, takes its name from the historic Cardozo Education Campus and offers a quieter counterpoint to U Street's evening energy — tree-lined blocks of rowhouses that house a mix of longtime families, Howard-affiliated residents, and newer renters drawn by the neighborhood's location and character.

The African American Civil War Memorial and adjoining museum, located directly at the corridor's Metro station, adds another layer of historical weight to the neighborhood, honoring the roughly 200,000 Black soldiers and sailors who served the Union during the Civil War. That memorial sits at the literal crossroads of U Street's jazz history and its civil rights history, reinforcing the corridor's identity as one of the most historically significant few blocks in the entire city.

Modern U Street continues to draw comparisons to its Black Broadway heyday, with venues like the Lincoln Theatre and Bohemian Caverns' successor spaces regularly hosting live jazz, comedy, and community events that consciously nod to the corridor's musical legacy. That blend of historical reverence and contemporary nightlife gives U Street a texture unlike any other DC neighborhood — equally comfortable hosting a jazz tribute night and a late-night dance party within the same block.

Best-Selling Cannabis Products in This Area

U Street's nightlife-driven energy shapes District Cure's sales patterns from this neighborhood in much the same way as Adams Morgan, with sativa and hybrid strains, pre-rolls, and vape cartridges topping the list for customers preparing for an evening out among the corridor's bars and music venues. Fast-acting edibles and beverages are increasingly popular here too, especially among younger professionals looking for an alternative to a night of drinking.

Because U Street also has a strong residential population in its surrounding rowhouses and newer apartment buildings, District Cure sees a healthy mix of both social-use and daily-wellness customers from this area. Full-size flower purchases and CBD-forward tinctures are popular among longer-term Cardozo residents, while pre-roll multi-packs and single-serving edibles dominate among younger renters and weekend visitors drawn to the nightlife.

Concentrates see solid demand from experienced U Street consumers who appreciate the neighborhood's overall culture of connoisseurship — a corridor built on jazz clubs and craft cocktail bars tends to attract customers who want the same level of quality and knowledge from their cannabis retailer. District Cure's staff report frequent conversations here about pairing specific strains with music, dining, or creative activities, a reflection of the neighborhood's artistic identity.

Topicals and CBD products see more modest demand in this corridor compared to residential neighborhoods farther out, consistent with U Street's identity as more of a destination and nightlife hub than a quiet, wellness-focused community — though the growing residential population along 14th and U Street continues to shift that balance.

Live music nights at the corridor's historic venues tend to correlate with visible upticks in pre-roll and edible sales at District Cure, particularly ahead of weekend shows. Staff have learned to anticipate these patterns around the neighborhood's concert calendar much the way a nearby restaurant might plan staffing around a big reservation night.

Legal Access & Medical Cannabis Registration

U Street's blend of DC residents, weekend visitors, and out-of-town concertgoers means District Cure regularly processes all three registration pathways for customers from this neighborhood: resident self-certification, out-of-state reciprocity, and temporary non-resident registration for first-time visitors. All three can typically be completed online in advance of a visit, letting customers shop the same day they arrive. Concertgoers heading to one of the corridor's music venues often register during the drive or Metro ride into the city, arriving at District Cure with their temporary digital registration already in hand. That level of preparation has become second nature for U Street's regular concertgoing crowd, many of whom plan a District Cure stop the same way they'd plan parking or dinner reservations.

Cannabis Use in Washington DC: By the Numbers

The U Street Corridor's storied jazz history parallels a broader cultural shift in how cannabis is regarded nationally — support for legalization now sits at roughly 87% of Americans, and DC's own registered medical patient population has climbed past 116,000. With DC's overall adult cannabis use rate near 33%, among the highest in the country, corridors like U Street — long associated with music, culture, and nightlife — reflect exactly the kind of social normalization researchers have tracked nationally as cannabis use has become more openly discussed and destigmatized. Nationally, cannabis added an estimated $149 billion to the economy in 2025, and the roughly 42% of active consumers who use daily or near-daily reflects the kind of routine, integrated use that corridors like U Street have long normalized through their music and social culture. It's a pattern with deep roots — jazz musicians and their audiences on this same strip were writing about cannabis in song lyrics generations before legalization was ever on the table, long before most of the country gave the topic serious thought or legal consideration, let alone the kind of regulated retail framework DC has today.
